# Epic Retro — Design Rationale

*Read this before changing the skill. SKILL.md is deliberately terse and literal; several of its choices look arbitrary and are not, and several things it does not do are deliberate refusals rather than omissions.*

## Governing Thought

**Epic Retro is an evidence-to-deltas compiler with a hard write-authority contract.** It mines what an unattended epic run leaves behind, verifies the result empirically, judges the epic against the contract it declared for itself, and lands every output in one of exactly two destinations — the repo (permanent, enforced) or the project spec layer (transient, scheduled death) — reserving human judgment for the few writes where error is unenforced and blast radius is high.

Five findings force this shape, and each anchors one section below:

1. **The problem is aggregate and the witnesses are perishable** — unattended epics accumulate defects invisible at any per-change scope, and the evidence to catch them exists but rots.
2. **Analysis must be built around derived views and per-epic contracts** — because aggregate defects cannot be seen in any reading of the diff, and no two epics are comparable.
3. **Only two write destinations are legitimate** — every artifact must have either enforcement or a death date; standing memory files have neither and are banned.
4. **Authority follows enforcement** — the machine may act where errors get caught, must ask where they don't, and may never touch process source without a human.
5. **Mechanism must not outrun incidents** — every self-regulation scheme we designed up front failed adversarial review; only the write-authority contract deserves early hardening.

---

## 1. The problem is aggregate and the witnesses are perishable

**The skill exists because per-session checks structurally cannot produce epic-level confidence, while the evidence that could is already on disk.**

- **The world:** a story is one coding session's intent plus context, sized by the usable context window (~100–150K tokens); an epic is consecutive sessions implementing something cohesive. Human attention concentrates at the epic's edges — design decisions are front-loaded into the first stories, the rest run unattended for hours.
- **The gap at the boundary:** the returning human has zero context and zero justified confidence. Each session was *validated* (its checks passed) but never *reviewed* — not for correctness, verification gaps, or fidelity to intent, at either session or epic scope.
- **The defect class that matters is emergent:** nine sessions each add three hundred innocent lines; no session ever saw the 3,000-line god class they collectively built. Intent drifts by quiet reinterpretation between sessions; two sessions solve the same problem two different ways. No per-change check can see this even in principle.
- **The old retrospective answered this world with theater.** It simulates an agile meeting — personas "remember" and contribute lessons. That was rational when nothing durable existed to consult; it is irrational now that an epic leaves a spec, story files, a full diff, per-story commits, review findings, and (usually) session logs. Simulating witnesses when you have witnesses wastes them. First commitment: **the phase is defined by what it reads and writes, not by what meeting it imitates.**
- **The witnesses rot.** Session logs — the only record of *why* session 4 took a strange turn, what was tried and abandoned — are the first evidence destroyed (compaction mid-run, retention after). The boundary is the one moment capturing them is cheap; every epic closed without doing so schedules a future archaeology dig.
- **Evidence availability varies, so honesty about coverage is structural.** Logs are sometimes missing entirely. Every downstream analysis declares what it needs and degrades honestly when it is absent rather than pretending, and the closeout trail records what ran and what was skipped — a later reader must be able to distinguish "checked and clean" from "never checked."

## 2. Analysis must be built around derived views and per-epic contracts

**Three design rules govern the Analyze phase: derive the aggregate, don't just read the diff; run the system, don't just review the change; judge each epic against its own declared contract, never against another epic.**

- **Aggregate views are the phase's unique contribution.** Architecture delta, duplication map, pattern divergence, size trajectories, spec-to-implementation reconciliation — these are properties of the whole that *no ordering of diff hunks can show*. They must be computed as first-class derived objects. Diff-scope review passes matter but exist in many tools — correctness weighted to the seams between stories precisely because no single session ever saw both sides of a seam, verification gaps, intent conformance; the aggregate views exist nowhere else (survey, July 2026: every commercial product — Devin Review, Codex, Copilot coding agent, Qodo, CodeRabbit, Greptile — operates at single-PR/single-task granularity; the multi-session boundary is unowned). Aggregate views are ordered before diff passes in the skill as a statement of priority, not dependency.
- **For verification gaps specifically, the strongest prior art is pre-AI.** [Teamscale Test Gap Analysis](https://teamscale.com/features/test-gap-analysis) crosses VCS change history with test-execution coverage; its Munich Re study found untested changes ~5× more defect-prone — the empirical case for making changed-behavior-without-tests a first-class finding category.
- **The decomposition trap is real and evidence-backed.** Controlled studies show small, well-decomposed changes are reviewed *well* ([di Biase et al. 2019](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7924728/): decomposition significantly cut false positives; [Baum et al. 2019](http://tobiasbaum.github.io/rp/memoryCodeOrderAndReview.pdf), n=50: effectiveness significantly higher for small changes). But those studies measure only defects local to the change under review — the paradigm cannot represent an aggregate defect. Decomposing the epic into small reviews would re-implement per-session blindness with nicer presentation: small changes are reviewed well, and those reviews are nearly useless against exactly the defect class this phase exists to catch.
- **Empirical verification is non-delegable.** Nothing else in the pipeline ever runs the system. Step 5 exercises the changed flows end-to-end; passing tests do not substitute. Its findings enter the same router as all others.
- **Epics are not remotely similar, so the analysis battery cannot be fixed.** A platform upgrade, a cohesive feature, and a bucket of small fixes have incommensurable profiles — for a refactoring, structural change is the *point* (drift analysis inverts) and behavior preservation is the whole correctness question; for a feature, intent conformance and cross-story seams dominate; for a bucket, convention divergence is the main aggregate risk. Hence the **audit plan**: the epic spec declares at spec time — when intent is explicit — what kind of epic this is, what acceptance means, which views matter, and what would count as failure. The boundary executes that contract (step 2); profiling the epic late is a retained fallback, a worse guess for epics whose specs predate the practice.
- **Structural enforcement belongs in-loop; the boundary keeps the judgment residue.** Practitioner consensus on fitness functions (ArchUnit, dependency-cruiser) correctly places deterministic checks inside the agent's verification loop. [SonarQube Architecture Management](https://www.sonarsource.com/blog/sonarqube-architecture-management-keeps-agent-generated-code-sound/) is the productized form — it reverse-engineers the actual dependency structure on every analysis and diffs it against a declared intended architecture, which is the pattern behind this skill's architecture-delta view. The boundary's structural job is what no allow-list expresses — god classes, pattern divergence — plus compiling what it learns into new deterministic gates for the next run.

## 3. Only two write destinations are legitimate

**The output law: every artifact must have either an enforcement mechanism or a scheduled death. Two destinations qualify; standing memory files have neither and are banned.**

- **The router feeds the destinations, and it is the novel core.** Each finding receives a *disposition* toward the product — fix now / defer / accept as-is — and, independently, a *lesson* toward the upstream artifacts: what would have prevented it (spec wording, story sizing, a missing convention or gate, or nothing). Orthogonal axes: what to do about this instance, and what to change so there is no next instance. The upstream-prevention inference has no prior art anywhere in the survey.

- **The repo — permanent because enforced.** Fixes, tests, CI/lint/architecture gates, consistency cleanups, expected-fail tests for known gaps, and commit messages carrying rationale and rejected alternatives. Everything here either executes or is fused to something that does. Lessons that matter forever must be expressed here, executably; a norm should graduate down the stack — prose → gate → structure that makes the violation inexpressible — until it costs zero attention and enforces itself.
- **The spec layer — transient because scheduled to die.** The project spec and companions, including the deferred-work file, hold lessons that matter for the remainder of the project. The layer dies with the project, bounding its rot by construction. Deferred work stays prose because most deferral is *intention, not decision* ("this module wants splitting" is not expressible as a test; narrow behavioral punts can be expected-fail tests, which are decisions). Deferred items must be re-claimed by an active plan at each re-triage or be dropped — no hoarding.
- **Standing memory files are banned** (no AGENTS.md-style lesson stores, memory.md, project-context.md as harvest targets). They are immortal prose with neither death nor enforcement — the artifact class whose signature is every pathology this design agonized over: bloat, staleness, self-reinforcing wrong norms, context collapse. A context file is a cache of comprehension; as reading source gets cheaper every quarter, the cache is pure staleness liability. The repo-as-memory alternative also inherits for free the provenance, journaling, revert, and bisect machinery a prose norm-base would need rebuilt — and it is the only memory format guaranteed readable by whatever agent tooling exists in eighteen months.
- **Consistency commits are first-class, not cosmetic.** Agents learn conventions by pattern-matching the codebase; a codebase that solves one problem three ways is corrupted teaching material. Canonicalizing divergence improves every future session — the code is the curriculum.
- **Supporting research:** [ACE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.04618) (ICLR 2026) names the failure modes of maintained context artifacts — *brevity bias* and *context collapse* — and prescribes incremental deltas over rewrites; the constraint survives here even though the artifact strategy radicalized past ACE's playbooks. [claude-devtools](https://github.com/matt1398/claude-devtools) proves session-log mining is tractable but is read-only — the persistence half (this skill's router) has no prior art at all.

## 4. Authority follows enforcement

**Write authority is proportional to how fast and loudly an artifact catches error. Three tiers; human input is solicited evidence, never orchestrated ceremony; and the skill cannot silently accept a failing epic.**

- **Design as if no human is involved.** Inputs are evidence; outputs are deltas. Human input enters as *solicited evidence*: pointed questions where an answer would materially change a delta, with evidence links, answerable in any amount or never — plus one open question for what the analyses missed. The skill never asks anyone to "review the epic"; humans review however they please. (The alternative — a curated review experience / narrative dossier — was designed in detail and cut: story-level HITL tooling had already failed ergonomically here (checkpoint-preview), epic scale is strictly harder, and the efficacy evidence for presentation-layer remedies is absent. Baum: "We cannot conclude reliably whether the order of presenting the code change parts influences the efficiency of code review"; [Bouraffa et al. 2025](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2506.10654) shows revealed *demand* — 44.6% of 23K mined reviews deviate from alphabetical order, and deviators cover more of the diff and approve less readily — not efficacy. [Devin Review](https://cognition.com/blog/devin-review) ships narrative diff review per-PR with zero independent efficacy evidence: built ≠ works.)
- **The tiers** (stated as the Write Authority section at the top of SKILL.md). Repo: machine may commit, and machine defaults may execute — tests, CI, and revert catch a wrong default. Spec layer: evidence-backed edits apply directly; *uncertain* edits require a human answer, and headless runs hold them as pending proposals — the spec layer has no immune system, and applying an uncertain interpretation "with a question attached" would launder machine judgment into the contract governing all remaining epics (worst around accepted deviations and re-planning, where the point of the question is that the answer could change the write). Skills: pull requests only, human merges, always — widest blast radius (every future epic), slowest feedback (weeks, confounded), no enforcement (a bad edit degrades silently); by the artifact law, the human gate *is* their grafted-on enforcement mechanism.
- **The acceptance gate is explicit and asymmetric.** The epic is judged (step 17) against its declared criteria on verification evidence and unresolved findings: accepted / accepted with open items / rejected. A human decision always overrides; the machine may default to accept only when criteria are demonstrably met. An epic failing its criteria with no human decision closes as *not accepted* — the one behavior the skill must be incapable of is silently accepting failure. The verdict sits after the lesson-bearing steps because lessons and re-planning are more valuable on a rejected epic, not less. Step 18 then records the verdict and the full trail — what ran, what was skipped, what remains open. (Design history: an early skeleton conflated cutting the presentation layer with cutting acceptance and closed the epic with no gate at all; that was flagged and fixed. Do not re-conflate them.)
- **Sub-agents generate hypotheses, never conclusions — and the recheck is graded by the same authority ladder.** The retro cannot be single-context (it reads more evidence than any epic session), so indexing, derivation, and review delegate to sub-agents. Their outputs are testimony: source refs and checked scope are required fields in the retro file, so a finding without them cannot be routed — structure enforcing what prose could only request. Before actuation, findings are regrounded per write-authority grade: the parent reopens primary sources for spec-layer and skill targets (no enforcement catches an error there); repo-bound fixes get their recheck from the remediation session itself, which touches the actual code and fails on contact with a hallucinated finding. Asymmetric review carries one special rule: independence cannot be faked inline — without sub-agents it degrades to prompt files a human runs in separate sessions, or an explicitly recorded skip, never to the parent role-playing independent reviewers in its own context.
- **Why defect-finding is the machine's job at all:** the review literature settled this before agents existed. [Czerwonka et al. 2015](https://www.microsoft.com/en-us/research/publication/code-reviews-do-not-find-bugs-how-the-current-code-review-best-practice-slows-us-down/): ~15% of review comments concern defects, mostly minor; Bacchelli & Bird 2013: review's real products were comprehension transfer and norm enforcement. In the agent world those two functions transform — comprehension reverses direction (no author to teach; the human needs the mental model), and norms stop being conversational (an agent never sees the PR comment again; norms must land in artifacts or gates). This skill is those two surviving functions rebuilt for agents, with defect-finding delegated to machines at the scope where machines are competent.

## 5. Mechanism must not outrun incidents

**Every self-regulation mechanism designed for this loop failed adversarial review before it shipped. The lesson is the epistemology: case law, not statutes — harden up front only the high-blast-radius contract (what may be written, where, and what always requires a human), and let everything else wait for its incident. Each stabilizer, when installed, cites the incident that motivated it.**

- **Four demolitions, one shared root cause.** (a) *Scoring deltas by recurrence of their finding class*: absence of recurrence is also the signature of a wrong-but-obeyed norm — the metric reads identically for health and pathology; epic-over-epic comparison is causally unidentifiable (n=1 each side, everything varies); base rates are too sparse to carry signal; the proposer would grade its own homework. (b) *Hard budgets with eviction*: eviction keyed on recency-of-incident removes the silently succeeding norms first (success is silence), their problems recur, they get re-adopted — a machine for oscillation; Chesterton's fence automated at scale. (c) *Vital-signs trending across epics*: epics are incommensurable; judging dissimilar efforts against each other's silhouettes is the classic metrics-regime error — measuring the shape of the work and attributing it to the worker. (d) *Promotion tiers with epoch-based hysteresis*: "confirmed across N consecutive epics" is meaningless when consecutive epics may never touch a norm's subject matter — confirmation would have to be exposure-relative, not calendar-relative, at which point the tier machinery adds nothing. Root cause throughout: **in a working norm system, success is silent; any mechanism keyed on observable activity inverts.**
- **What survived — as design tests, not machinery:** (1) no mechanism may read silence as failure or activity as value; (2) an anomaly is never itself a finding — it is a question addressed to the evidence, and only an answered question becomes a finding; (3) judgment is always against the epic's own declared contract; (4) nothing the loop writes is exempt from the loop's scrutiny (step 6 explicitly checks whether findings trace to a previous run's own output — a loop whose products escape its own review converges on self-consistent nonsense). Exactly one hard rule survived: **no skill modification without human review** — also the cheapest, one line about who holds the pen.
- **The accounting caution, with its one valid exception.** Building the full control system before the first transaction is the naive-systematizer move — accounting insights applied to software management. The kept disanalogy: stateless agents, unlike the humans metrics regimes misjudge, have *no tacit layer* — everything a future session knows arrives through artifacts. Writing things down is not the accounting mistake; deriving judgment from counts of what was written is.
- **Iteration strategy: backtest, and build disposable.** Epics complete roughly weekly; learning from live runs alone would take a year the field will not sit still for. Instead: (a) run the retro retroactively on *completed* epics, whose futures are known — which deferred items bit, which drift hardened, which defects escaped — validating the analysis half against ground truth, rerunnable many times a day, decoupled from epic cadence; (b) separate what ages fast from what ages slowly — the skill, its prompts, and its routing are disposable (lifetime months, rebuild cost a day; never encode procedure where model judgment will improve past it, and never build machinery whose validation period exceeds its lifetime), while the repo — including its gates and commit-message record — is the only accumulating asset, which is also why it is the primary delta destination.
- **Remediation is bounded by construction.** Fix-now findings become story-shaped work executed by *the same dev loop that built the epic* (no second, bespoke code-changing mechanism), one round only, then fast checks and re-driven flows — without a convergence bound the phase can loop on its own output indefinitely.
- **Sub-agent instructions live at the point of use, never in a preamble.** This skill accumulates large contexts; a rule stated at the top of the file is out of recall by the time it is needed, and there is no single global policy to state anyway — each delegation has its own fallback (derivation degrades to inline computation; asymmetric review never degrades inline; remediation hands to the dev loop, which owns its own policy). When this skeleton shards into step files, the delegation contract goes in the step that delegates and the consumption contract in the step that consumes — quick-dev is the house pattern (interactive, capability-tolerant, per-step fallbacks, and "disregard sub-agent severity" stated at the consumption point); dev-auto's refuse-without-sub-agents stance is wrong for an interactive skill.
- **Resumability is artifact-based and phase-grained — needed by construction, not by elegance.** The workflow stops by design: questions wait for humans, remediation runs for hours, evidence arrives late. Single-shot execution is unrealistic, so a run may stop at any phase boundary and resume there. State lives in one per-epic retro file in the spec layer — frontmatter status (last completed phase, open questions), body accumulating each phase's products — which doubles as step 18's closeout record: the state file and the record are the same artifact, dying with the scaffolding per the artifact law, so no second memory system is invented. Because the file can drift from the world (commits land, questions get answered elsewhere, the spec changes), every run begins by reconciling file against reality, and reality wins: analyses may be recomputed, routing revised on new evidence, writes reconciled before reapplying — never duplicated. Coarse phase-level status, no opaque session state, no workflow engine inside the workflow. This also makes backtesting cleaner: a historical epic's retro file is just a retro file that starts at phase 1.

## Naming

The skill succeeds `bmad-retrospective` and will take over the "retro" name: the name marks the *slot* in the delivery lifecycle, and slots outlive mechanisms. The anthropomorphic meeting dies; the retro is dead, long live the retro. `bmad-retro-new` is a temporary passport until the old skill is deprecated.

## Known Debts

- **Confidence packaging is unsolved, deliberately.** The acceptance gate exists (steps 5, 17), but nothing renders its evidence for comfortable human consumption. If a dossier returns, it returns as an optional layer over this skill's outputs, instrumented as a hypothesis (e.g., occasional seeded defects to measure reviewer miss rate — Baum's method turned on ourselves).
- **Reject mechanics are underspecified.** What follows "not accepted" — revert, branch abandonment, re-planning scope — is left to the human and the spec layer. Deliberate for now: rejection should be rare, and its mechanics depend on branch/merge conventions this skill does not own.
- **The old retrospective still exists** with a nearly identical trigger phrase; deprecation is pending.
- **The audit plan depends on the spec-authoring skills** growing a "how this epic will be judged" section; until then, step 2's fallback profiling carries the load.
- **Session-log mining depth is unspecified.** The evidence is rich (context blowouts, retry storms, repeated convention violations map to story-sizing and tooling lessons); keep implementations shallow until backtesting shows which process findings route to useful deltas.
